Workflow · APN / parcel number

Find a property owner by APN or parcel number

5 min read · By the LeadCove team

You have an APN. Maybe it came from a foreclosure filing, a tax-lien sale, a county export, or a piece of vacant land with no street address. You need to find the property and the owner. Here's the workflow.

What an APN is

The Assessor's Parcel Number (APN), also called the parcel ID or PIN, is the unique identifier the county assigns to a piece of real estate. Every property in every U.S. county has one. The format varies by county:

  • California: dash-separated blocks (e.g., 123-456-789)
  • Florida: long numeric string (e.g., 01-2345-678-0000)
  • Texas: often alphanumeric with prefix codes
  • New York: block-lot format (varies by borough)
  • Most other states: a county-specific numeric or alphanumeric format

The APN is stable across address changes, owner transfers, and time. The street address might change when a street is renamed or renumbered; the APN doesn't.

The lookup workflow

Step 1 — Identify the county

APNs are assigned by individual counties, not centrally. The same APN format can refer to different properties in different counties. You need to know which county the APN belongs to. If the context where you got the APN doesn't tell you, the format usually hints at the state, and from there a property data platform can search across the state's counties.

Step 2 — Search the county property portal

Every county property appraiser or assessor portal supports APN search alongside address search. Paste the APN. The result includes:

  • The property's street address
  • The recorded owner name and mailing address
  • Assessed value and tax history
  • A link to the recorded deed (in most counties)
  • Property characteristics (lot size, year built where applicable, building square footage)

Step 3 — Resolve the owner to a phone and email

The county record gives you the owner's name and mailing address. To actually reach them, you need their current phone and email — which county records don't include. Either run the address through an owner-data tool, or check consumer records sites manually for one-off lookups.

When APN lookup beats address lookup

  • Vacant land. Undeveloped lots often have no street address. The APN is the only identifier.
  • Foreclosure or tax-sale lists. County foreclosure filings list properties by APN; the street address sometimes appears as a separate field, sometimes not.
  • County-data exports. Bulk property exports from a county assessor use APN as the primary key. Working from APN forward is faster than reverse-mapping addresses.
  • Disputed or renumbered streets. The APN is unambiguous; the address sometimes isn't.

Shortcut. If you already have the property's street address, looking up by address is faster than APN. APN lookup is for the cases where you don't have an address, or where the address is ambiguous. FAQ

What is an APN or parcel number?

Assessor's Parcel Number — the unique identifier a county assigns to a piece of real estate. Format varies by county. Stable across address changes and owner transfers.

How do I find a property by APN?

Search the relevant county property appraiser or assessor portal by APN. The result shows the street address, recorded owner, assessed value, and tax history. From there, an owner-data tool resolves the owner to verified phone and email.

Can I find the owner from just an APN with no county info?

You need the county. APN formats often hint at the state; a property-data platform can search across the state's counties simultaneously when needed.

When would I look up by APN instead of by address?

Vacant land, foreclosure or tax-sale lists, county-data exports, disputed or renumbered street situations.
